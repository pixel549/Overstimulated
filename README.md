# Overstimulated

Overstimulated is a top-down household chaos game about a dad trying to hold it together for 2 increasingly hostile days. You survive by triaging chores, navigating a crowded house, and keeping the overstimulation meter below 100%.

This README is the feature and design source of truth for the current project. Older progress notes, patch notes, and implementation checklists have been folded into this document.

## Current Scope

- The shipped scope is 2 days, not 5.
- Day 1 should feel serious, readable, and survivable with good play.
- Day 2 should feel hilariously unmanageable once Munty arrives.
- The game is designed for desktop first, but it also supports phone-sized layouts and touch controls.
- Built-in and external playtest bots exist for iteration, telemetry, and regression checks.

## Core Fantasy

- You are not trying to perfect-clear the house. You are trying to stay functional inside a reactive mess.
- The house is a spatial puzzle. Doors, corridors, patios, dogs, and slow NPCs all affect route quality.
- Calm-down tools matter, but they cost time and positioning.
- NPCs are not pure helpers. Even the "friendly" ones create pressure, traffic, noise, and follow-on chores.
- Day 2 is not a fair escalation. It is active opposition.

## Controls

### Keyboard

- `WASD` or Arrow Keys: move
- `Shift`: sprint
- `E` or `Space`: quick action / interact / confirm
- Hold `E` or `Space`: progress longer hold tasks
- `Q`: bark
- `R`: interact with NPCs
- `\`: toggle debug overlay

### Built-in Playtest Panel

- `P`: start or stop autoplay
- `[`: cycle playtest speed
- `]`: export telemetry JSON
- `0`: restart the run

### Touch

- Phone-sized layouts expose on-screen movement and action buttons for play.

## Win And Lose

- Each day lasts a few minutes.
- You survive by completing enough work to stay on top of the chaos.
- If overstimulation reaches 100%, Dad storms off and the run ends.

## Design Pillars

### 1. Reactive chaos

- Chores are generated by the house, NPC behavior, and timed events.
- The player should feel pulled in several directions at once.
- Systems should interact in messy ways: doors, dogs, baby, chickens, and timed chores should compound pressure.

### 2. Spatial pressure

- Pathing matters.
- Tight connectors, doors, and moving NPCs should change the cost of a decision.
- The house should feel readable but never frictionless.

### 3. No perfect solution

- The correct play is usually prioritization, not full completion.
- Calm-down actions should be powerful enough to matter, but costly enough to create tradeoffs.

### 4. Day identity

- Day 1 teaches the house and asks the player to take the task board seriously.
- Day 2 weaponizes the same systems and adds Munty plus aggressive chickens.

## Current Gameplay Structure

Every run is built from the same 5 buckets.

### 1. Procedural chores

These are generated from house state and NPC behavior.

- Chicken poop on patio spaces
- Toys and living-room clutter
- Wife-assigned chores
- General cleanup and maintenance tasks
- Baby and dog pressure incidents

### 2. Routine chores

These are timed anchors in the day.

Currently implemented or represented in the live game:

- Dishes / sink chores
- Sprinkler timing events
- Beer time
- Chicken curfew and chicken panic pressure
- Refill dog water and other recurring home tasks

Still part of the design target:

- Stronger scheduled dog-feeding rhythm
- More explicit recurring household beats that make each day feel structured

### 3. Routine interactables

These are repeatable environmental tools or rituals.

- Couch and bed relaxation
- Toilet hiding
- Coffee machine
- Beer pickup / use window
- NPC social interaction
- Mower pickup and grass-cutting

### 4. Actual RNG

- Dogs barking or reacting unexpectedly
- Baby wake / baby mood variation
- Toy or mess generation
- NPC traffic changes that alter safe routes

### 5. Daily challenge layer

- Day 1: baseline home chaos
- Day 2: Munty plus aggressive chickens plus stronger pressure stacking

## Task Model

The game uses a few chore shapes rather than one generic task.

### Fetch tasks

- Go to a point of interest and interact with it
- Examples: beer, dog water, picking something up, checking a location

### Hold tasks

- Stay committed in place to finish
- Examples: dishes, baby-related care, other time-cost chores

### Coverage tasks

- Sweep an area or repeat an action until the zone is handled
- Examples: mowing and area cleanup

The point is to force different rhythms: quick taps, route-heavy jobs, and "you are stuck here now" decisions.

## Stress And Recovery Systems

### Stress goes up from

- Too many active chores
- Old chores sitting unresolved
- Ambient household pressure
- Nearby chaos sources
- Sprinting and noisy actions in the wrong moment

### Stress goes down from

- Standing still briefly
- Deliberate breathing / pausing
- Completing tasks
- Using calm-down interactables well

### Calm-down tools

- `Relax spots`: couch, bed, reading chairs, and similar furniture
- `Toilet hiding`: strongest emergency calm-down tool, with dramatic camera treatment
- `Coffee`: temporary stress resistance buff
- `Beer`: timed relief beat with its own daily window

## House And Spatial Layout

The house is split into distinct rooms, connectors, patios, and yards.

Key spaces:

- Living room: central traffic and clutter
- Kitchen: sink, coffee, common chores
- Reading / dog room: quieter side room and dog-related pressure
- Baby's room: cot and baby tasks
- Master bedroom and ensuite: stronger calm-down spaces
- Housemate room, office, spare room: right-side routing and traffic friction
- Patio and patio strip: chicken mess and outdoor transitions
- Dog patio and dog yard: bowls, mower, outdoor dog pressure
- Chicken yard, run, coop, shed: bird chaos, curfew, beer, and yard chores

Those files are still useful because they are map references, not feature-design docs.

## Navigation And World Rules

- Walls and doors are real blockers.
- Doors are intended to matter for both routing and household chaos.
- NPCs use waypoint-based navigation for reliable room-to-room movement.
- Chickens use smarter flee behavior so they do not just pin into walls forever.
- The player, NPCs, and chores should all compete for the same readable physical space.

## Interactions

### Bark

Bark is a context-sensitive tool, not just flavor.

- Near adults: sends them to the nearest task and grants a 25% assist
- Near dogs: excites and redirects them toward Dad
- Near baby: gets attention and may change baby state
- Near chickens: scatters them

### NPC interaction

- `R` is the social interaction key
- Used for talking, petting, and low-pressure contextual responses
- Meant to add readability, flavor, and occasional slight relief

### Mower

- Can be picked up and carried
- Slows movement while equipped
- Cuts grass patches while moving outdoors

## Character Design

### Wife

- Productive, mobile, and sometimes helpful
- Should feel like a partial ally who still creates chaos by existing
- Can assist tasks when barked toward them
- Can also add chores or disrupt the player's clean plan

### Jake

- Slower and retreat-prone
- Occupies awkward parts of the right-side house
- Helps when barked, but should still feel like social and spatial friction

### Baby

- The supervision pressure source
- Creates route constraints and emotional urgency
- Toys, tantrums, attention demands, and nap logic are all part of the intended pressure profile

### Dogs

Momo and Piper are not just mascots. They are moving obstacles, bowl-race agents, and relaxation disrupters.

- Dog bowls create synchronized movement and chokepoints
- Dogs should pull the player's attention around the house
- Momo is clingier and more underfoot
- Piper is faster and more independent

### Chickens

- Regular chickens create poop, motion, and panic
- Aggressive chickens turn Day 2 into a worse routing problem
- Chicken flock / flee / coop behavior is part of the house's outdoor rhythm

### Munty

Munty is the Day 2 signature system.

Current live behavior:

- Spawns on Day 2
- Chases people chaotically
- Vibrates and pulses visually
- Ignores normal fence expectations
- Makes the house feel actively more hostile

Still-valid design target:

- Munty should sabotage calm-down routines and punish complacency
- Day 2 should feel intentionally unreasonable once he arrives

## Playtesting And Debugging

### In-game autoplay

- Use the playtest panel in the page, or press `P`
- Exports JSON telemetry for stuck points, completed tasks, and event timelines
- Good for repeatable sanity checks and watching route quality

You can also autostart it with:

```text
http://127.0.0.1:8000/?autoplay=1&speed=2
```

### External direct-player bot

- Lives in `direct_player_bot.py`
- Drives the game externally through browser automation
- Saves reports to `playtest_reports`
- Useful when comparing "internal autoplay" against an outside controller

### Debug overlay

- `\` toggles the in-game debug layer
- Intended for fast world checks, not as the main player-facing UI
